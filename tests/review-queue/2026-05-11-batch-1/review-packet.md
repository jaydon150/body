## 1. eighth rib (rib 8)

- **Structure ID:** `UBERON:0010757`
- **FMA alias:** `FMA:8120`
- **TA Latin (record):** *costa VIII*
- **Authored by:** Content agent, P1.15 dispatch
- **Confidence (current):** `pending`

### Summary

The eighth rib (costa VIII) is a false rib of the thoracic cage. It articulates posteriorly with the bodies of the seventh and eighth thoracic vertebrae and joins the costal margin anteriorly by attaching its costal cartilage to that of the seventh rib rather than directly to the sternum.

### Long form

The eighth rib (Latin: costa VIII) is one of twelve paired bones of the thoracic cage and the second of the so-called false ribs (costae spuriae). Like the other typical ribs, it has a head, neck, tubercle, body, and costal cartilage.

The head bears two articular facets that meet the costal facets on the bodies of the seventh and eighth thoracic vertebrae across the intervening intervertebral disc. The neck connects head to tubercle; the tubercle articulates with the transverse process of T8 at the costotransverse joint. The body curves laterally, then forward and downward, and carries a costal groove along its inferior internal margin in which run the intercostal vein, artery, and nerve.

Anteriorly the eighth rib does not reach the sternum directly. Its costal cartilage articulates with the costal cartilage of the seventh rib, contributing to the costal margin. This indirect attachment defines the eighth rib as a vertebrochondral or false rib. The rib forms part of the boundary of the thoracic cage and gives attachment to several of the intercostal muscles and the upper fibres of the diaphragm.

## 2. Femur

- **Structure ID:** `UBERON:0000981`
- **FMA alias:** `FMA:9611`
- **TA Latin (record):** *os femoris*
- **Authored by:** Content agent, P1.15 dispatch
- **Confidence (current):** `pending`

### Summary

The femur (os femoris) is the longest and strongest bone of the human body. It forms the skeleton of the thigh and articulates proximally with the acetabulum of the hip bone and distally with the tibia and patella at the knee.

### Long form

The femur (Latin: os femoris) is a single long bone that constitutes the skeleton of the thigh. It is the longest, strongest, and heaviest bone in the body and transmits force from the trunk and pelvis to the tibia during stance and locomotion.

The proximal end carries a near-spherical head that articulates with the acetabulum of the os coxae to form the hip joint. The head is connected to the shaft by a narrowed neck, which is angulated with respect to the shaft (the neck-shaft angle); this geometry shifts the line of weight transmission laterally and is a frequent site of fracture in the elderly. At the junction of neck and shaft project the greater trochanter laterally and the lesser trochanter medially, both serving as insertion sites for muscles that act on the hip.

The shaft (corpus femoris) is roughly cylindrical with a posterior longitudinal ridge, the linea aspera, which gives attachment to the adductors of the thigh and the short head of biceps femoris. Distally the shaft expands into medial and lateral condyles separated posteriorly by the intercondylar fossa; the condyles articulate with the corresponding tibial condyles, and an anterior patellar surface receives the patella. The medial and lateral epicondyles project from the condyles and serve as the proximal attachments of the collateral ligaments of the knee.

Ossification begins from a primary centre in the shaft during fetal life and proceeds through five secondary centres (head, greater and lesser trochanters, and the two distal condyles), with the distal centre typically present at birth.

## 3. mandible

- **Structure ID:** `UBERON:0001684`
- **FMA alias:** `FMA:52748`
- **TA Latin (record):** *mandibula*
- **Authored by:** Content agent, P1.15 dispatch
- **Confidence (current):** `pending`

### Summary

The mandible (mandibula) is the lower jaw bone, the only mobile bone of the adult skull. It carries the lower dental arch and articulates bilaterally with the temporal bones at the temporomandibular joints.

### Long form

The mandible (Latin: mandibula) is the largest and strongest bone of the facial skeleton and the only freely movable bone of the adult skull. It develops from paired halves that fuse at the symphysis menti shortly after birth, producing a single horseshoe-shaped bone.

The mandible consists of a horizontal body and two vertical rami that meet the body at the angles of the mandible. The body bears the alveolar process superiorly, which houses the lower teeth, and the mental protuberance anteriorly. Each ramus terminates superiorly in two processes separated by the mandibular notch: the anterior coronoid process, which receives the temporalis muscle, and the posterior condylar process, whose head articulates with the mandibular fossa of the temporal bone to form the temporomandibular joint.

The internal surface of the body shows the mylohyoid line, marking the origin of the mylohyoid muscle, while the lateral surface of the ramus gives insertion to the masseter and the medial surface to the medial pterygoid. The inferior alveolar nerve and vessels enter the mandibular foramen on the medial aspect of the ramus, traverse the mandibular canal, and emerge anteriorly through the mental foramen as the mental nerve.

## 4. ethmoid bone

- **Structure ID:** `UBERON:0001679`
- **FMA alias:** `FMA:52740`
- **TA Latin (record):** *os ethmoidale*
- **Authored by:** Content agent, P1.15 dispatch
- **Confidence (current):** `pending`

### Summary

The ethmoid bone (os ethmoidale) is an unpaired bone of the cranial base between the orbits. It contributes to the medial wall of each orbit, the roof and lateral wall of the nasal cavity, and the floor of the anterior cranial fossa.

### Long form

The ethmoid bone (Latin: os ethmoidale) is a light, spongy bone situated between the two orbits and at the roof of the nasal cavity. It consists of four parts: a horizontal cribriform plate, a vertical perpendicular plate, and two lateral ethmoidal labyrinths.

The cribriform plate forms part of the roof of the nasal cavity and the floor of the anterior cranial fossa. Numerous foramina perforate it and transmit the olfactory nerve filaments (CN I) from the olfactory mucosa to the olfactory bulb. The midline crista galli projects superiorly from the cribriform plate and serves as the anterior attachment of the falx cerebri.

The perpendicular plate descends from the cribriform plate and contributes to the superior part of the nasal septum. Each lateral labyrinth contains the ethmoidal air cells (anterior, middle, and posterior), which open into the superior and middle meatuses of the nasal cavity. The medial wall of each labyrinth gives rise to the superior and middle nasal conchae, which project into the nasal cavity, while the lateral wall forms the orbital plate (lamina papyracea) — a thin sheet that constitutes most of the medial wall of the orbit.

## 5. frontal bone

- **Structure ID:** `UBERON:0000209`
- **FMA alias:** `FMA:52734`
- **TA Latin (record):** *os frontale*
- **Authored by:** Content agent, P1.15 dispatch
- **Confidence (current):** `pending`

### Summary

The frontal bone (os frontale) is an unpaired bone of the neurocranium that forms the forehead and the bony framework of the roof of each orbit and the anterior wall of the anterior cranial fossa.

### Long form

The frontal bone (Latin: os frontale) is the anterior bone of the cranium. Developmentally it arises from paired halves that normally fuse at the metopic suture during early childhood; the adult bone is therefore usually a single midline structure.

It has three principal parts. The squamous part (squama frontalis) is a curved plate that forms the forehead and the anterior part of the calvaria; it bears the paired frontal eminences laterally and the supraorbital margins inferiorly. The orbital parts (partes orbitales) are two horizontal triangular plates that form the larger portion of the roofs of the orbits and the floor of the anterior cranial fossa. The nasal part lies in the midline between the orbital parts and articulates inferiorly with the nasal bones and frontal processes of the maxillae.

Within the bone, on each side of the midline above the orbits, lies a frontal sinus — an air-filled cavity that drains into the middle meatus of the nasal cavity. The frontal bone articulates posteriorly with the parietal bones at the coronal suture, laterally with the greater wings of the sphenoid and the zygomatic bones, and inferiorly with the ethmoid, lacrimal, nasal, and maxillary bones.

## 6. maxilla

- **Structure ID:** `UBERON:0002397`
- **FMA alias:** `FMA:9711`
- **TA Latin (record):** *maxilla*
- **Authored by:** Content agent, P1.15 dispatch
- **Confidence (current):** `pending`

### Summary

The maxilla is a paired bone of the facial skeleton. The two maxillae fuse in the midline to form the upper jaw, the floor of the orbits, the lateral walls and floor of the nasal cavity, and most of the hard palate.

### Long form

The maxilla (Latin: maxilla) is the largest paired bone of the facial skeleton. The right and left maxillae meet at the intermaxillary suture and together form the upper jaw and contribute to the orbital, nasal, and oral cavities.

Each maxilla has a body and four processes: frontal, zygomatic, palatine, and alveolar. The body is roughly pyramidal and contains the maxillary sinus (antrum of Highmore), the largest of the paranasal sinuses, which drains into the middle meatus of the nasal cavity. The frontal process extends superiorly to articulate with the frontal bone; the zygomatic process projects laterally to meet the zygomatic bone; the palatine process extends medially and meets its fellow in the midline to form the anterior two thirds of the hard palate; and the alveolar process bears the maxillary teeth.

The orbital surface of the body forms most of the floor of the orbit and is grooved by the infraorbital canal, which transmits the infraorbital nerve (a branch of the maxillary nerve, V2) and vessels to emerge anteriorly at the infraorbital foramen. The nasal surface forms the lateral wall of the nasal cavity and bears the maxillary hiatus. The maxilla articulates with nine bones: the frontal, ethmoid, nasal, zygomatic, lacrimal, inferior nasal concha, palatine, vomer, and the maxilla of the opposite side.

## 7. occipital bone

- **Structure ID:** `UBERON:0001676`
- **FMA alias:** `FMA:52735`
- **TA Latin (record):** *os occipitale*
- **Authored by:** Content agent, P1.15 dispatch
- **Confidence (current):** `pending`

### Summary

The occipital bone (os occipitale) is an unpaired bone at the posteroinferior part of the cranium. It surrounds the foramen magnum and articulates with the atlas (C1) at the atlanto-occipital joints.

### Long form

The occipital bone (Latin: os occipitale) forms the posterior and most of the inferior wall of the cranium. It is conventionally divided into four parts arranged around the foramen magnum: the squamous part posteriorly, the two lateral (condylar) parts on either side, and the basilar part anteriorly. These parts are separate at birth and fuse during childhood.

The foramen magnum transmits the medulla oblongata as it becomes continuous with the spinal cord, the vertebral arteries, and the spinal accessory nerves. On either side of the foramen, the lateral parts bear the occipital condyles, which articulate with the superior articular facets of the atlas; nodding movements of the head occur at these atlanto-occipital joints. The hypoglossal canal pierces the lateral part above each condyle and transmits the hypoglossal nerve (CN XII).

The external surface of the squamous part bears the external occipital protuberance and the superior nuchal line, marking the attachments of the trapezius, occipitofrontalis (occipital belly), and other posterior cervical muscles. The internal surface shows the cruciate eminence, with grooves for the superior sagittal sinus and transverse sinuses; the basilar part fuses anteriorly with the body of the sphenoid in early adulthood to form the clivus.

## 8. parietal bone

- **Structure ID:** `UBERON:0000210`
- **FMA alias:** `FMA:9613`
- **TA Latin (record):** *os parietale*
- **Authored by:** Content agent, P1.15 dispatch
- **Confidence (current):** `pending`

### Summary

The parietal bone (os parietale) is a paired bone of the neurocranium that forms most of the side and roof of the cranial vault. The two parietal bones meet in the midline at the sagittal suture.

### Long form

The parietal bone (Latin: os parietale) is a flat, curved, quadrilateral bone of the cranial vault. The two parietals together form the largest portion of the calvaria and articulate with one another in the midline at the sagittal suture.

Each parietal bone has an external (convex) and an internal (concave) surface and four borders. Anteriorly it articulates with the frontal bone at the coronal suture, posteriorly with the occipital bone at the lambdoid suture, and inferiorly with the squamous part of the temporal bone and the greater wing of the sphenoid. The external surface bears the parietal eminence (the most prominent point of the bone), and is crossed by two arched lines — the superior and inferior temporal lines — which mark attachments of the temporalis muscle and its fascia.

The internal concave surface is grooved by branches of the middle meningeal artery and, near the sagittal margin, lodges the superior sagittal sinus in a midline groove formed jointly with the contralateral parietal bone. Pacchionian (arachnoid) granulations indent the bone in small depressions adjacent to the sagittal sulcus.

## 9. sphenoid bone

- **Structure ID:** `UBERON:0001677`
- **FMA alias:** `FMA:52736`
- **TA Latin (record):** *os sphenoidale*
- **Authored by:** Content agent, P1.15 dispatch
- **Confidence (current):** `pending`

### Summary

The sphenoid bone (os sphenoidale) is a single, complex bone at the centre of the cranial base. It articulates with all the other cranial bones and contributes to the floor of the middle cranial fossa.

### Long form

The sphenoid bone (Latin: os sphenoidale) is an unpaired, butterfly-shaped bone wedged between the frontal, ethmoid, occipital, temporal, and parietal bones. It articulates with every other bone of the cranium and forms a substantial portion of the cranial base.

It has a central body and three pairs of processes: the greater wings, the lesser wings, and the pterygoid processes. The body contains the sphenoidal sinuses and bears the sella turcica on its superior surface, with the pituitary fossa (hypophyseal fossa) lodging the pituitary gland. The lesser wings project laterally from the upper part of the body, contribute to the floor of the anterior cranial fossa, and form the superior margin of the superior orbital fissure. The greater wings form most of the floor of the middle cranial fossa and bear three important foramina: the foramen rotundum (transmitting the maxillary nerve, V2), the foramen ovale (mandibular nerve, V3), and the foramen spinosum (middle meningeal artery).

The pterygoid processes descend from the junction of body and greater wings; each consists of a medial and lateral plate that give attachment to the pterygoid muscles and the pharyngeal constrictors. Between the lesser wing, greater wing, and body, the superior orbital fissure transmits the oculomotor, trochlear, ophthalmic (V1), and abducens nerves and the superior ophthalmic vein.

## 10. temporal bone

- **Structure ID:** `UBERON:0001678`
- **FMA alias:** `FMA:52737`
- **TA Latin (record):** *os temporale*
- **Authored by:** Content agent, P1.15 dispatch
- **Confidence (current):** `pending`

### Summary

The temporal bone (os temporale) is a paired bone of the neurocranium that forms part of the lateral wall and base of the skull. It houses the organs of hearing and balance and articulates with the mandible at the temporomandibular joint.

### Long form

The temporal bone (Latin: os temporale) is an irregular paired bone forming the inferolateral part of the cranium and a portion of the cranial base. Each temporal bone is conventionally described as having four principal parts that fuse during development: the squamous, petromastoid (petrous and mastoid), and tympanic parts, together with the styloid process.

The squamous part is a thin plate that contributes to the lateral wall of the cranium and articulates with the parietal bone above. Its anterior projection, the zygomatic process, meets the temporal process of the zygomatic bone to form the zygomatic arch. Inferior to the zygomatic process lies the mandibular fossa, which receives the head of the mandible at the temporomandibular joint, with the articular tubercle marking its anterior boundary.

The petrous part is a dense, pyramidal portion of the cranial base that contains the internal ear (cochlea, vestibule, and semicircular canals) and the internal acoustic meatus, which transmits the facial and vestibulocochlear nerves. The mastoid part lies posterior to the external acoustic meatus and projects inferiorly as the mastoid process, an attachment site for the sternocleidomastoid, splenius capitis, and longissimus capitis. The tympanic part forms much of the wall of the external acoustic meatus. The styloid process projects inferoanteriorly and serves as an attachment for stylohyoid and stylopharyngeus muscles and the stylohyoid and stylomandibular ligaments.

## 11. atlas (C1)

- **Structure ID:** `UBERON:0001092`
- **FMA alias:** `FMA:12519`
- **TA Latin (record):** *atlas*
- **Authored by:** Content agent, P1.15 dispatch
- **Confidence (current):** `pending`

### Summary

The atlas (C1) is the first cervical vertebra. It is a ring-shaped bone without a body, articulating superiorly with the occipital condyles to support the cranium and inferiorly with the axis (C2) at the atlanto-axial joints.

### Long form

The atlas (Latin: atlas) is the first cervical vertebra. Named for the Titan who, in Greek mythology, supports the heavens, it bears the weight of the cranium and is unique among vertebrae in lacking both a body and a true spinous process.

The atlas consists of an anterior arch, a posterior arch, and two lateral masses. The lateral masses bear the superior articular facets, which are concave and receive the occipital condyles to form the atlanto-occipital joints — the principal joints of head flexion and extension. The inferior articular facets are more nearly flat and articulate with the superior facets of the axis at the lateral atlanto-axial joints.

The anterior arch is short and bears the anterior tubercle on its convexity; on its posterior surface lies a small facet that articulates with the dens of the axis. The posterior arch is longer and represents the homologue of the laminae of typical vertebrae; it bears the posterior tubercle, a rudimentary spinous process, and is grooved superiorly by the vertebral artery as it ascends to enter the foramen magnum. The transverse processes are long and easily palpated; the transverse foramen in each transmits the vertebral artery and veins.

The transverse ligament of the atlas stretches across the vertebral foramen behind the dens, dividing the canal into an anterior compartment for the dens and a posterior compartment for the spinal cord and meninges.

## 12. axis (C2)

- **Structure ID:** `UBERON:0001093`
- **FMA alias:** `FMA:12520`
- **TA Latin (record):** *axis*
- **Authored by:** Content agent, P1.15 dispatch
- **Confidence (current):** `pending`

### Summary

The axis (C2) is the second cervical vertebra. Its distinguishing feature is the dens (odontoid process), which projects superiorly from the body and forms the pivot around which the atlas, and with it the head, rotates.

### Long form

The axis (Latin: axis) is the second cervical vertebra. It is the strongest of the cervical vertebrae and bears the dens (Latin: dens axis, odontoid process), a tooth-like vertical projection from the superior surface of the body that represents, developmentally, the centrum of the atlas fused to the body of the axis.

The dens articulates anteriorly with the posterior surface of the anterior arch of the atlas at the median atlanto-axial joint and is held in position by the transverse ligament of the atlas, the apical ligament, and the paired alar ligaments. Rotation of the head — the 'no' movement — occurs principally at this joint.

The body of the axis carries a sloping anterior surface and is fused with the dens above. Superior articular facets are large and lie on the body and pedicles, sloping laterally to receive the inferior facets of the atlas. Inferior articular facets lie on the laminae and articulate with C3 in the typical manner of cervical zygapophyseal joints. The bifid spinous process is large and palpable. The transverse processes are short and bear transverse foramina, which transmit the vertebral artery.

## 13. cervical vertebra 7 (C7)

- **Structure ID:** `UBERON:0004616`
- **FMA alias:** `FMA:12525`
- **TA Latin (record):** *vertebra cervicalis VII*
- **Authored by:** Content agent, P1.15 dispatch
- **Confidence (current):** `pending`

### Summary

The seventh cervical vertebra (C7), or vertebra prominens, marks the transition from the cervical to the thoracic spine. Its long, non-bifid spinous process is readily palpable and serves as a surface landmark for counting vertebral levels.

### Long form

The seventh cervical vertebra (Latin: vertebra cervicalis VII), also called vertebra prominens, is a transitional vertebra between the cervical and thoracic regions. Its most conspicuous feature is its long spinous process, which is typically non-bifid and ends in a single tubercle that is easily palpable at the base of the neck — a standard surface landmark for vertebral counting.

Like other cervical vertebrae, C7 has a small body, a triangular vertebral foramen, and transverse processes that contain a foramen transversarium. The foramen transversarium of C7 is smaller than that of C1-C6 and typically transmits only an accessory vertebral vein; the vertebral artery itself usually enters the cervical column at C6, so the foramen of C7 does not carry the artery in most individuals.

The transverse processes are larger and posteriorly placed, sometimes possessing only a posterior tubercle. The ventral ramus of C7 joins the brachial plexus as part of the middle trunk (with C7 alone) or as a contribution to the upper trunk in pre-fixed plexus variants. A cervical rib, when present, arises from the costal element of the C7 transverse process and may compress the lower trunk of the brachial plexus.

## 14. sacrum

- **Structure ID:** `UBERON:0003690`
- **FMA alias:** `FMA:16202`
- **TA Latin (record):** *os sacrum*
- **Authored by:** Content agent, P1.15 dispatch
- **Confidence (current):** `pending`

### Summary

The sacrum (os sacrum) is a large triangular bone at the base of the vertebral column, formed by the fusion of five sacral vertebrae. It is wedged between the two hip bones and forms the posterior wall of the pelvis.

### Long form

The sacrum (Latin: os sacrum) is a triangular bone at the base of the vertebral column, formed by the fusion of the five sacral vertebrae during late adolescence and early adulthood. It transmits the weight of the upper body to the pelvis and lower limbs through the sacroiliac joints.

The base of the sacrum, formed by the superior surface of the first sacral vertebra (S1), faces upward and slightly forward and articulates with the body of L5 at the lumbosacral joint. The anterior projection of the body of S1 — the promontory — is an important obstetric landmark. The apex of the sacrum lies inferiorly and articulates with the coccyx.

The pelvic (anterior) surface is concave and smooth; four pairs of pelvic sacral foramina transmit the ventral rami of the upper four sacral nerves. ### Summary

The talus is the second largest tarsal bone and the most superior. It transmits the weight of the body from the tibia to the rest of the foot and contributes to the ankle, subtalar, and talocalcaneonavicular joints.

### Long form

The talus (Latin: talus, ankle bone) is the most superior bone of the tarsus and the only bone of the foot to articulate with the leg bones. It transmits the entire weight of the body from the tibia to the talocalcaneonavicular complex and then to the rest of the foot.

### Long form

The first thoracic vertebra (Latin: vertebra thoracica I) is the most superior of the twelve thoracic vertebrae and the immediate inferior neighbour of the vertebra prominens (C7). - **TA Latin (record):** *vertebra thoracica XII*
- **Authored by:** Content agent, P1.15 dispatch

### Summary

The twelfth thoracic vertebra (T12) is the most inferior of the thoracic vertebrae and a transitional vertebra. It has a single complete costal facet on each side of its body for the twelfth rib, and its inferior articular processes face laterally in the lumbar pattern.

### Long form

The twelfth thoracic vertebra (Latin: vertebra thoracica XII) is the lowest of the thoracic vertebrae and exhibits transitional features at the thoracolumbar junction. Its body is comparable in size to that of a small lumbar vertebra. Each side of the body bears a single, complete costal facet for the head of the twelfth rib, without demifacets above or below, reflecting the absence of articulation between the twelfth rib and any neighbouring vertebra.

The transverse processes are short and lack costal facets — the twelfth rib articulates only with the body, not the transverse process — and present small superior, inferior, and lateral tubercles that correspond to the mammillary, accessory, and costal processes of lumbar vertebrae respectively. The spinous process is shorter and more horizontal than at mid-thoracic levels.

The superior articular facets of T12 face posteriorly and laterally in the thoracic pattern. The inferior articular facets, however, face laterally and slightly anteriorly in the lumbar pattern, articulating with the superior articular facets of L1 at this transitional level. This switch in facet orientation occurs abruptly within a single vertebra in approximately half of individuals and contributes to the susceptibility of the T12-L1 junction to rotational injury.

### Long form

The sixth thoracic vertebra (Latin: vertebra thoracica VI) is a representative mid-thoracic vertebra. The body is roughly heart-shaped in superior view and bears, on each lateral surface, two demifacets: a superior demifacet articulating with the head of the sixth rib and an inferior demifacet articulating with the head of the seventh rib. - **Authored by:** Content agent, P1.15 dispatch

### Summary

The tibia is the medial and larger of the two long bones of the leg. It transmits weight from the femur to the talus and forms, with the fibula, the bony framework of the leg.

### Long form

The tibia (Latin: tibia, flute or shinbone) is the medial and larger long bone of the leg and the second-largest bone in the human body after the femur. It transmits the weight of the body from the femur to the talus and contributes to the knee joint proximally and the ankle joint distally.

The proximal end is expanded into medial and lateral condyles separated by the intercondylar eminence, on either side of which lie the anterior and posterior intercondylar areas, where the cruciate ligaments and the menisci attach. The superior surfaces of the condyles are the tibial articular surfaces (tibial plateau) that meet the corresponding condyles of the femur. The lateral condyle bears a small facet on its inferolateral aspect for the head of the fibula (proximal tibiofibular joint).

The shaft is triangular in cross-section, with anterior, medial, and interosseous (lateral) borders and corresponding surfaces. The anterior border, sharp and subcutaneous, is palpable as the shin; the medial surface is also subcutaneous along its whole length. The tibial tuberosity on the upper part of the anterior surface receives the insertion of the patellar ligament. The interosseous border gives attachment to the interosseous membrane that connects tibia and fibula.

The distal end is smaller and bears a square inferior articular surface that articulates with the trochlea of the talus at the ankle joint. The medial malleolus projects inferiorly from the medial side of the distal end as a subcutaneous prominence; the fibular notch on the lateral side receives the distal end of the fibula at the inferior tibiofibular joint.

### Summary

The ulna is the medial of the two long bones of the forearm. It is the principal articulating bone with the humerus at the elbow but, distally, does not directly participate in the wrist joint.

### Long form

The ulna (Latin: ulna) is the medial long bone of the forearm, lying on the little-finger side of the forearm in the anatomical position. It is the principal articulating bone with the humerus at the elbow but is excluded from the wrist joint proper by an intervening articular disc.

The proximal end is massive and forms the major portion of the elbow joint. The trochlear (semilunar) notch on the anterior surface is a concavity that articulates with the trochlea of the humerus. The tuberosity of the ulna lies just distal to the coronoid process and gives insertion to brachialis.

The shaft tapers from proximal to distal and is triangular in cross-section, with anterior, posterior, and medial surfaces. The lateral surface is sharp and bounded by the interosseous border, to which the interosseous membrane is attached.

The distal end is small and rounded, comprising a head and a styloid process. The head bears a circumferential articular surface for the ulnar notch of the radius and an inferior surface that articulates with the triangular fibrocartilage articular disc (which separates it from the carpus). The styloid process projects from the posteromedial aspect of the head.

### Summary

The xiphoid process (processus xiphoideus) is the smallest, most inferior part of the sternum. It articulates superiorly with the body of the sternum at the xiphisternal joint and serves as an attachment site for the rectus abdominis and parts of the diaphragm.

### Long form

The xiphoid process (Latin: processus xiphoideus, sword-shaped process) is the small, variable cartilaginous projection that forms the most inferior part of the sternum. It remains largely cartilaginous in early life and ossifies progressively from above downward, often only partially even in adulthood.

The xiphoid process articulates superiorly with the body of the sternum at the xiphisternal joint, a symphysis that typically ossifies by middle age. The xiphisternal joint corresponds in surface anatomy to the inferior limit of the heart, the central tendon of the diaphragm, and the T9 vertebral level. The tip of the xiphoid is highly variable: it may be pointed, blunt, bifid, or curved anteriorly or posteriorly.

The anterior surface gives attachment to the medial fibres of the rectus abdominis and the aponeurosis of the internal oblique. The posterior surface gives attachment to the lower fibres of the transversus thoracis and to a portion of the diaphragm. The lateral borders give attachment to the seventh costal cartilage (its lower part) and the linea alba below.

### Summary

The zygomatic bone (os zygomaticum) is a paired bone of the facial skeleton that forms the prominence of the cheek and contributes to the lateral wall and floor of the orbit and to the zygomatic arch.

### Long form

The zygomatic bone (Latin: os zygomaticum), commonly called the cheekbone, is a paired, roughly quadrangular bone lying between the maxilla, the frontal bone, the sphenoid, and the temporal bone. It contributes to the prominence of the cheek, to the lateral wall and inferolateral margin of the orbit, and to the zygomatic arch.

Each zygomatic bone has three surfaces (lateral, temporal, and orbital) and four processes. The frontosphenoidal process ascends to articulate with the zygomatic process of the frontal bone and the greater wing of the sphenoid at the lateral wall of the orbit. The maxillary process meets the zygomatic process of the maxilla anteroinferiorly. The temporal process projects posteriorly to meet the zygomatic process of the temporal bone, completing the zygomatic arch. The orbital surface contributes to the lateral wall and floor of the orbit.

The lateral surface is pierced by the zygomaticofacial foramen, transmitting the zygomaticofacial nerve and vessels; the temporal surface bears the zygomaticotemporal foramen for the corresponding nerve. The zygomatic bone gives partial origin to the masseter muscle from its lateral surface and inferior border.
